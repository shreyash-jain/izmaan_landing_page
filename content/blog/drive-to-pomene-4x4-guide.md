The short answer is **yes — you need a 4×4.** The longer answer is worth reading before you book your flights, because the drive to Pomene is the single thing first-time visitors most often underestimate.

This is the honest version: what the road is actually like, why tyre pressure matters more than horsepower, how long it really takes, and what to do if you can't bring the right vehicle.

## Key takeaways

- **A high-clearance 4×4 is essential** — the final sand sections are non-negotiable.
- **It's about flotation, not power** — lower your tyre pressure (≈1.2–1.4 bar) for the sand.
- **~106 km from Vilankulo**, but plan a **half-day** with stops.
- **Arrive in daylight.** Never tackle the sand tracks after dark on a first visit.
- **No 4×4? Arrange a transfer** with your lodge before booking flights.

## The route from Vilankulo

Most journeys start in **Vilankulo**, the nearest town with an airport. From there the road to Pomene comes in three rough stages:

1. **Tar** — the easy part, on the main road heading south.
2. **Graded gravel** — slower, corrugated in places, but manageable.
3. **Soft sand** — the final approach into the reserve, and the section that decides everything.

It's that last stretch of deep, soft sand that a standard car simply cannot handle.

## Why tyre pressure matters more than power

Here's the part people get wrong. Most visitors who get stuck in Pomene aren't underpowered — they're **over-inflated.**

Soft sand rewards a large, flat tyre footprint. Letting your tyres down dramatically increases that footprint and lets the vehicle *float* over the surface instead of digging in. As a general starting point, dropping to around **1.2–1.4 bar** transforms how a 4×4 handles the sand — but always confirm the right figure for your specific vehicle, tyres and load, and **re-inflate** once you're back on firm ground.

Bring (or confirm your vehicle has):

- A reliable **tyre pressure gauge**.
- A way to **re-inflate** (compressor) for the return leg.
- Basic recovery awareness — and ideally a travelling companion vehicle.

> Most people who get stuck in Pomene aren't underpowered — they're over-inflated.

## How long does it take?

From Vilankulo, plan on **the better part of a day** with stops. The distance — about 106 km — is misleading; the surface sets the pace, not the odometer. Build in time for lowering tyre pressure, photo stops and the simple fact that you won't (and shouldn't) rush the sand.

Most importantly: **arrive in daylight.** The tracks are unmarked, and judging soft sand in the dark on a first visit is exactly how easy trips become long nights.

## Can a 2×4 ever make it?

Honestly, no — not reliably, and not without risking an expensive, time-consuming recovery. The occasional confident local in exactly the right conditions is the exception, not a plan you should bank a holiday on.

If you can't arrange a suitable 4×4, you have a much better option: a **transfer.** Arrange it with your lodge before you book flights. It's far cheaper to plan the journey properly than to be towed out of a dune — and it lets you enjoy the scenery instead of white-knuckling the wheel.

## A simple pre-trip checklist

- [ ] High-clearance **4×4** confirmed (rental or own).
- [ ] **Tyre gauge + compressor** on board.
- [ ] **Full fuel** and **cash** — services are sparse out here.
- [ ] **Daylight arrival** planned.
- [ ] Lodge briefed on your **ETA and vehicle** so they can guide you in.
